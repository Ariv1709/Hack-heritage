Executive: Articles 52-78

Article 52: The Constitution establishes the President of India as the head of the executive branch of the government.

Article 53: The President holds all executive powers and can delegate these powers to the Prime Minister and other officials.

Article 54: The President is elected by an Electoral College consisting of members of both houses of Parliament and the Legislative Assemblies of States and Union territories.

Article 55: Details the manner of election of the President and the process of determining the votes.

Article 56: The President holds office for a term of five years but is eligible for re-election.

Article 57: Provides for the conditions for the re-election of the President.

Article 58: Outlines the qualifications needed to be elected as President, including being a citizen of India and at least 35 years old.

Article 59: Describes the conditions under which the President can be removed from office, including impeachment for violation of the Constitution.

Article 60: The President takes an oath to preserve, protect, and defend the Constitution.

Article 61: Provides the procedure for the impeachment of the President, requiring a two-thirds majority in both houses of Parliament.

Article 62: Specifies that elections for the President should be held within six months of the end of the term or earlier if the office becomes vacant.

Article 63: Provides for the office of the Vice President, who is also the ex-officio Chairman of the Rajya Sabha.

Article 64: Details the powers and duties of the Vice President, including acting as the President in the latter's absence.

Article 65: Provides the Vice President's role as Acting President when the office of the President is vacant.

Article 66: Outlines the procedure for the election of the Vice President.

Article 67: Specifies the conditions under which the Vice President can be removed from office.

Article 68: Details the circumstances under which the Vice President acts as the President.

Article 69: Provides for the oath of office to be taken by the President, Vice President, and other high officials.

Article 70: Deals with the functions of the President in the event of the dissolution of the Lok Sabha.

Article 71: Details the procedure for resolving disputes relating to the election of the President and Vice President.

Article 72: Provides the President with the power to grant pardons, reprieves, and commutations of sentences.

Article 73: Outlines the extent of the executive powers of the Union government and its relationship with the states.

Article 74: Describes the powers and functions of the Council of Ministers headed by the Prime Minister.

Article 75: Specifies the process for the appointment and removal of the Prime Minister and other ministers.

Article 76: Provides for the appointment of the Attorney General of India and their duties.

Article 77: Details the conduct of business by the Union government and its ministries.

Article 78: Outlines the responsibilities of the Prime Minister to inform the President about the affairs of the government.

Article 79: Establishes the Parliament of India, consisting of the Lok Sabha and the Rajya Sabha.

Article 80: Defines the composition of the Rajya Sabha, including members appointed by the President and elected members from states and union territories.

Article 81: Outlines the composition of the Lok Sabha, based on the population of the states and union territories.

Article 82: Provides for the delimitation of constituencies and the allocation of seats to states based on population.

Article 83: Specifies the term of office for members of the Lok Sabha (five years) and Rajya Sabha (six years).

Article 84: Defines the qualifications required to be a member of Parliament, including citizenship and age.

Legislative Powers of the President: Article 123

Article 123: The President has the power to issue ordinances when Parliament is not in session. These ordinances have the same force as laws but must be approved by Parliament within six weeks of reassembly.

The Union Judiciary: Articles 124-147

Article 124: Establishes the Supreme Court of India, the highest court in the country, responsible for interpreting the Constitution and adjudicating disputes.

Article 125: Provides for the salaries and allowances of the Supreme Court judges, which are determined by Parliament and cannot be reduced during their term of office.

Article 126: Details the appointment of an Acting Chief Justice if the Chief Justice is unable to perform duties.

Article 127: Allows for the appointment of additional judges to the Supreme Court to handle an increase in workload.

Article 128: Provides for the appointment of retired judges as ad-hoc judges to the Supreme Court if necessary.

Article 129: Declares the Supreme Court as the guardian of the Constitution and the highest authority in matters of constitutional law.

Article 130: Defines the seat of the Supreme Court and its jurisdiction over the whole of India.

Article 131: The Supreme Court has original jurisdiction to hear disputes between the Government of India and one or more states or between states.

Article 132: The Supreme Court has appellate jurisdiction over matters involving the interpretation of the Constitution.

Article 133: The Supreme Court has appellate jurisdiction in civil cases where the matter in dispute involves a substantial question of law.

Article 134: The Supreme Court has appellate jurisdiction in criminal cases where there has been a conviction and the accused claims a violation of their rights.

Article 135: Provides the Supreme Court with jurisdiction to enforce fundamental rights and other constitutional provisions.

Article 136: Allows the Supreme Court to grant special leave to appeal from any judgment, decree, or order of any court or tribunal.

Article 137: Provides the Supreme Court with the power to review its own judgments and orders.

Article 138: Allows the Supreme Court to hear appeals in certain cases from any court or tribunal other than those mentioned in the Constitution.

Article 139: Provides for the transfer of cases from one court to another by the Supreme Court.

Article 140: Allows the Supreme Court to issue directions or orders for the implementation of its judgments.

Article 141: Declares that the law declared by the Supreme Court is binding on all courts within the territory of India.

Article 142: Empowers the Supreme Court to pass any order necessary to do complete justice in any matter or case.

Article 143: Provides the President with the authority to refer questions of law or public importance to the Supreme Court for its opinion.

Article 144: Requires all authorities, civil and judicial, to act in aid of the Supreme Court in the enforcement of its orders and judgments.

Article 145: Specifies the procedure for the conduct of business in the Supreme Court, including the number of judges and their powers.

Article 146: Provides for the appointment of the Chief Justice and other judges of the Supreme Court and their conditions of service.

Article 147: Details the powers of the Supreme Court to frame rules and regulations for its own functioning and procedure.

Comptroller and Auditor-General of India: Articles 148-151

Article 148: Establishes the office of the Comptroller and Auditor-General (CAG) of India, who is responsible for auditing government accounts and ensuring financial accountability.

Article 149: Defines the powers and duties of the CAG, including auditing the accounts of the Union and State governments and any other authority or body receiving government funds.

Article 150: Requires the CAG to submit reports on the accounts to the President and the State Governors, who are then required to present these reports to the respective Legislatures.

Article 151: Specifies the procedure for the presentation and examination of audit reports by Parliament and State Legislatures.
